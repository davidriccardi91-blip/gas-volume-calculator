04

What the materials are

★ The three you'll meet most
  • ★ Copper — AS 1432 Type Bverified
    AS 1432, Copper tubes for plumbing, gasfitting and drainage applications. Your standard, most-common copper. Type B is the medium wall grade (A thickest, C thinnest) — general-purpose gas, above or below ground. The grade the gas standard sizes on.
  • ★ Steel — AS 1074 Mediumverified
    AS 1074, Steel tubes and tubulars for ordinary service — Medium wall grade. The standard threaded gas pipe. Black or galvanised share the same bore, so one row covers both — galv is only a zinc coating.
  • ★ PE-X — gas PEX / Duopexconfirm
    AS 2492, Cross-linked polyethylene (PE-X) pipes; gas fittings to AS 2537.5. Your common flexible gas plastic — the coil you run above ground and in walls. Not in Table D.4.1, so L/m here is computed from the bore and must be confirmed against the pipe spec. If it's a multilayer PEX-AL-PEX, use the manufacturer's bore.
Others in Table D.4.1
  • Copper — NZS 3501 (NZ)verified
    NZS 3501, Specification for copper tubes for water, gas and sanitation. The New Zealand copper, slightly different bores.
  • Polyethylene (PE) — AS/NZS 4130confirm
    AS/NZS 4130, Polyethylene (PE) pipes for pressure applications — series 2, SDR 11. The buried poly main/service (black, yellow stripe). This is NOT your PEX — different material from the cross-linked PE-X above.
  • PVC-HI — AS ISO 6993.1confirm
    AS ISO 6993.1, Buried high-impact PVC (PVC-HI) piping for gaseous fuels — to max 1 bar (100 kPa). Buried gas only.
  • Polyamide (PA / nylon) — AS 2944.1confirm
    AS 2944.1, polyamide pressure pipe for gaseous fuels — SDR 25 (thin wall). Buried gas; uncommon in domestic work. Confirm the exact standard title against your copy.
05

How it's worked out

You don't work any of this out by hand — the tool does it as you fill the rows. All it's doing is:

  1. You pick the material and size — the tool fills in the volume per metre. That figure comes from Table D.4.1 in the standard and is already built in, so there's no separate chart to look at.
  2. You type the length — that section's volume is the per-metre figure times the length.
  3. It adds up every section — main run plus all branches — to give the total volume.
  4. The total sets your test time — 5 minutes up to 30 L, plus another 5 minutes for every extra 30 L.

The only time a formula comes into it is a pipe that isn't in the list. Then volume per metre = 0.0007854 × bore² (bore in mm, off the pipe spec). Everything in the dropdown already has its figure.

06

Working it out by hand

to check the tool

Two routes, same answer. Either one lets you sanity-check the tool without it.

Route A — straight off the chart, no formula
  1. Read each pipe size's litres-per-metre off Table D.4.1 (AS/NZS 5601.1) — the L/m chart by material and size.
  2. Multiply that L/m by the length of that size, in metres.
  3. Add every section — main run plus branches — for the total volume.
Route B — from the pipe bore (for anything not in the chart, e.g. PEX)
  1. Get the internal bore in mm — off the pipe spec, or AS/NZS 3500.1 Table A.1–A.3 (internal diameters by material).
  2. Volume per metre = 0.0007854 × bore × bore. The 0.0007854 is just π ÷ 4 ÷ 1000.
  3. Then multiply by length and add the sections, the same as Route A.
Worked example — copper Type B

12 m of DN20 (bore 17.0 mm): 0.0007854 × 17 × 17 = 0.227 L/m → × 12 = 2.72 L
4 m of DN15 (bore 10.9 mm): 0.0007854 × 10.9 × 10.9 = 0.093 L/m → × 4 = 0.37 L
Total = 2.72 + 0.37 = 3.09 L → under 30 L → 5-minute test

Off Table D.4.1 instead (0.22 and 0.09 L/m) you'd get 3.0 L — the same to within rounding. The chart is rounded to two decimals; the tool works from the exact bore, so a few thousandths' difference is rounding, not a fault.

Test time, by hand

Up to 30 L → 5 min.
Over 30 L → divide the total by 30, round up to the next whole number, × 5 min.
Example — 65 L: 65 ÷ 30 = 2.17 → round up to 3 → 3 × 5 = 15 min.
